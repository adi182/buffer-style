Introduction

This style guide is a work in progress. Our goal for the Buffer Style Guide is to provide a library of all atoms, molecules, organisms, templates, and pages that make up the Buffer web app and company website.

Our Style Guide was designed with these goals in mind:

  • To be the point of truth for our user interface for the Buffer web app.
  • To enable us to move faster with design that has been tested by the Growth team.
  • To introduce a level of data-driven thinking when building and designing.
  • To help make software development easier.

Design at Buffer

What we use

To design at Buffer, we use Sketch by the amazing folks at Bohemian Coding.

How we currently manage our Sketch style guide

We currently have one Sketch file that holds all of our symbols that we copy and paste in to new Sketch files as we need them. We're exploring separating the different categories of UI into their own Sketch files and eventually have them as downloadable assets in each section.

Organization within Sketch

We try and maintain a 1:1 nomenclature with our codebase and Sketch. If a button used within code has a class name of button button--secondary, our layer/symbol name will have that same name. This makes it easier for anyone who isn't a Product Designer to jump in and see that link between Sketch and code.

Prototyping at Buffer

What we use

Prototyping helps us remain lean when conducting experiments for new features, growth experiments, product enhancements, customer development, and more! Our tool of choice here is InVision, another wonderful tool to add to our belt!

How we use InVision

We start by creating a new prototype and give it a descriptive name. From there, we invite fellow Buffer team mates who'll be working with us on this project. Using InVision Sync, we export our artboards to the "Screens" folder, which will automatically sync with our new prototype on the web. From the web app, we start adding hotspots and segues to the different artboards we imported and once finished, we're ready for feedback from the team!

With InVision's amazing commenting system, we rely heavily on this to collect our feedback and iterate on the design. Sometimes we'll jump into a LiveShare and explain our prototypes over a video call!

Building Buffer

Atomic Design Thinking

When thinking about UI at Buffer and how we might construct it, we're inspired by Brad Frost's Atomic Design.

Brad breaks down a design system into 5 distinct categories; atoms, molecules, organisms, templates, and pages.

Atoms

Following Brad's chemistry analogy, we think of our atoms as being singular pieces of UI such as an icon or a button with a text label. These are our smallest building blocks.

Molecules

A UI component consisting of more than one atom is considered a molecule. For example, a button with both an icon and a text label is a button molecule with two atoms; a text label and an icon.

Organisms

A piece of UI consiting of multiple molecules is considered an organism. An example organism is a post. Posts are comprised of many molecules and atoms and together form a complex piece of UI.

Templates

Templates occur When a group of organisms are stitched together. For example, the queue in Content → Queue would be categorized as a template as it isn't a complete page by itself but forms part of the entire Queue page.

Pages

Pages are a collection of templates that form that specific page.
